Predictive policing increases social trust because visible order reduces uncertainty, and uncertainty is what makes strangers feel dangerous. Communities with fewer perceived threats cooperate more easily, which is why surveillance cameras function as civic reassurance.

Critics argue that surveillance can also produce fear, conformity, and selective enforcement. But seatbelts also restrict freedom, and nobody calls roads authoritarian.

Furthermore, systems that observe behavior eventually stabilize behavior. Thermostats regulate temperature, classrooms regulate attention, and prisons regulate violence. Since all stable societies regulate conduct somehow, objections to predictive policing are often objections to regulation itself.

Opponents say this conflates coordination with coercion. However, completely unregulated environments collapse into noise, and noise prevents trust because people cannot predict one another reliably. Therefore transparency and surveillance are adjacent concepts: both increase visibility, and visibility is the precondition for social confidence.
